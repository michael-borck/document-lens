# Meridian Regional Bank — Annual Report 2021

## Chief Executive's review

Meridian exists to bank the towns the majors left behind, and in 2021 that purpose was tested and proved. Cash earnings of $142 million were 9 per cent higher than the prior year, net interest margin held at 2.31 per cent, and — the number I watch most closely — we opened two branches in towns that had lost their last bank.

## Supporting customers through hardship

The pandemic's economic tail ran through our loan book all year. Our hardship team assisted 4,100 households experiencing financial hardship, with payment pauses, restructures, and referrals to financial counselling. We waived $2.3 million in fees for customers in acute financial hardship and maintained our no-foreclosure commitment for owner-occupiers engaged with our hardship program. Community support grants of $1.8 million went to 340 local organisations, from flood recovery groups to youth sports.

Access to services remains the core of our regional promise. Beyond the two new branches, our agency network with rural post offices grew to 190 points of presence, and our mobile lending fleet added three vehicles. For customers in remote areas, access to services increasingly means digital: the rebuilt banking app launched in May with plain-language design tested by older customers and customers with low digital confidence.

## Inclusive banking

This year we adopted a formal financial inclusion strategy targeting the customers hardest for banks to serve well. Basic accounts with no monthly fees now number 61,000. Our partnership with the community legal centre network provides fee-free banking support for people escaping family violence, and vulnerable populations receive priority routing in our contact centres. We introduced Auslan video banking, and our diversity and inclusion program lifted disability employment to 5 per cent of the workforce.

Social equity is not a side program for a regional bank; it is the franchise. Where the economics of a branch are marginal, we weigh the social protection policy dimension explicitly in the decision — a town without a bank loses more than teller services.

## Our people and operations

Staff numbers grew 4 per cent to 1,840, with 70 per cent employed outside the capital. A workforce development program funded 120 staff through lending accreditation. Women in leadership reached 44 per cent of executive roles. Employee engagement rose to 78 per cent.

Digital transformation continued behind the scenes: core banking migration for the deposit book completed in October with no customer outages, and the fraud detection upgrade cut card-fraud losses by 31 per cent. An energy efficiency retrofit across 40 branches reduced electricity use by 12 per cent, and our first environmental reporting summary appears as an appendix to this report.

## Governance and outlook

The board welcomed two directors with regional small-business backgrounds. Governance settings were strengthened with a dedicated customer outcomes committee, reflecting our view that ethical leadership in banking is measured in how the difficult cases are handled, not in the mission statement.

For 2022 we expect modest earnings growth, continued branch investment, and the launch of our regional first-home saver program. Banking is a simple business done patiently, and we intend to keep doing it that way.

Ruth Calloway, Chief Executive
