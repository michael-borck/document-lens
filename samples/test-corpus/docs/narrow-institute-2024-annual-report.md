# Narrow Waters Institute — Annual Report 2024

## Director's report

The Narrow Waters Institute exists for one purpose: the scientific care of the estuary and the catchment that feeds it. Everything in this report — every program, every grant, every measurement — serves that purpose. We are a small organisation by design, and we go deep rather than wide.

## The estuary program

Marine research remained the institute's largest program. The seagrass survey completed its twelfth annual transect series, recording the first meadow expansion since monitoring began — 14 hectares of new growth in the eastern basin, which we attribute to the sediment controls installed upstream in 2021. Ocean health indicators tracked by the mooring array held steady through the summer heat event, though the February readings reinforce how narrow the margin has become. Our coastal sustainability advice guided the shire's foreshore works at three sites, replacing rock armour with vegetated buffers on our recommendation.

## Rivers and wetlands

The wetlands team expanded the fish-passage study to four barriers and confirmed breeding by the threatened galaxias in the restored reach — the clearest signal yet that the water ecosystems of the upper catchment are responding to intervention. Water quality monitoring now runs continuously at nine stations, with the telemetry network upgraded this year; all data is public within 24 hours. Our water conservation partnership with the four irrigation districts completed its channel-lining trial, returning an estimated 3.1 gigalitres annually to environmental flows, and sustainable water use covenants now apply across 60 per cent of the catchment's licensed extraction.

## Land and species

The revegetation crews planted 118,000 seedlings across the escarpment corridors, the largest ecological restoration season in the institute's history, with third-year survival at 79 per cent. Biodiversity conservation outcomes included two new populations of the spider orchid and the return of brush-tailed phascogales to the northern block, confirmed by the camera array. Our land management agreements with 41 farming families now cover 9,200 hectares of remnant vegetation under permanent protection, and the fire-mosaic trial entered its fourth season. Environmental stewardship grants distributed $410,000 to landholder projects, from dam fencing to paddock-tree protection.

## Climate

The institute's climate resilience assessment for the estuary was delivered to the state government in July: sea-level scenarios, saltmarsh migration corridors, and the acquisition priorities needed to let the wetlands move inland. Emissions reduction within our own operations continued — the field fleet is now two-thirds electric and the research station runs on the microgrid year-round — and our climate strategy for the decade ahead pairs that operational work with the science program's central question: how much heat, and how fast, the estuary's environmental sustainability can withstand.

## The year ahead

Next year brings the deepening of the mooring array, the second stage of ecological restoration on the escarpment, and the saltmarsh acquisition case to government. The institute will remain small, and the estuary will remain the whole of our work.

Dr Ines Barreto, Director
