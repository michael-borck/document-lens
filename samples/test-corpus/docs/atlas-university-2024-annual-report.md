# Atlas University — Annual Report 2024

## Vice-Chancellor's introduction

Atlas serves 41,000 students across two campuses and one of the most diverse catchments in the country. This report is organised around our four functions — teaching, research, engagement, and operations — and within each we account for our economic, social, and environmental performance. Governance disclosures, including council attendance and executive remuneration, appear in the final section.

## Teaching and learning

Teaching excellence was recognised nationally this year with two citations and the university's best-ever student experience survey results. Educational access remains the defining commitment of our teaching mission: 28 per cent of commencing students are first in family, and our equity in education programs — bridging courses, regional study hubs, and needs-based scholarships worth $19 million — underpin that number. Inclusive education practice is now embedded through universal design standards applied to all new course materials, and digital learning options carried 31 per cent of total course load, extending educational access to students in work and in remote communities.

Our teaching also serves economic ends deliberately. Graduate employability rose to 87 per cent employed within four months, supported by 6,200 work-integrated learning placements. Graduate pathways into the region's health, engineering, and agriculture employers are formalised through curriculum co-design, and lifelong learning enrolments — micro-credentials and short courses for the existing workforce — grew 44 per cent. Environmental themes run through the curriculum too: the sustainability major doubled its intake, and climate strategy case studies are now core in the business school.

Student wellbeing anchors the social dimension of teaching: mental health support waiting times fell to two days, health promotion campaigns reached every faculty, and campus safety investments — lighting, escorts, and a redesigned reporting service — cut incident reports by 18 per cent.

## Research

Research income reached $312 million. Research infrastructure investment included the new genomics platform and stage two of the materials characterisation facility, and our researchers secured 14 fellowships in the national schemes.

Research addresses each pillar directly. Health research strengths in rural health and infectious disease attracted the year's largest grants, and health equity studies shaped two state programs. Environmental research delivered: marine research at the coastal station informed the harbour restoration, ocean health monitoring under our long-term observing program entered its fifteenth year, and the ecology group's biodiversity conservation work now guides three catchment authorities; water ecosystems research secured the new national wetlands node. Agricultural innovation from the dryland farming institute lifted trial yields by 11 per cent under reduced water, contributing to food security in the region's cropping systems, and research in food sciences produced the fortified flour adopted by the national aid program. Economic contribution flows through innovation and entrepreneurship: the accelerator supported 34 ventures, technology transfer income doubled, and innovation hubs at both campuses now house 61 companies employing 480 people.

## Engagement

Community partnerships define Atlas's civic role: 240 active agreements span schools outreach, the legal clinic, and the multicultural festival the university has hosted for 22 years. Urban engagement work with the city council shaped the transport corridor plan, and our public space development partnership converted the old rail yard into a shared campus-community precinct. Civic engagement programming — public lectures, the policy forum, citizen science — drew 60,000 participants.

Indigenous engagement is governed by the Elders' council: 460 Indigenous students enrolled (a record), the Indigenous research unit gained its first endowed chair, and cultural competency training reached 78 per cent of staff. Access for underrepresented groups extends through the prison education program and the refugee scholarship scheme, and our disability inclusion partnership with regional providers placed 90 students in internships. Multicultural initiatives include the community language schools we host each weekend.

International collaboration spans 89 partner institutions; global engagement this year focused on the Pacific, where capacity building programs trained 340 health workers and teachers. Strategic partnerships with the state government and two industry consortia fund the clean manufacturing institute, and cross-sector alliances on regional water security bring together farmers, utilities, and our researchers.

## Operations

Operations carry our environmental commitments. Emissions reduction reached 41 per cent below 2015 following the second solar array and full electrification of the campus bus fleet; carbon neutrality is targeted for 2030 with an audited transition plan. Energy efficiency projects across 30 buildings cut consumption 8 per cent. Campus water management earned the state award: sustainable water use practices — recycled irrigation, smart metering, drought-rated landscaping — cut potable demand 23 per cent, and water conservation is designed into every new building through our green infrastructure standards. Waste reduction hit 81 per cent diversion, environmental stewardship of the campus bushland reserve is managed with the traditional owners, and sustainable campus planning principles govern the 2030 estate masterplan. Our procurement policy weighted sustainability at 20 per cent in all major tenders, and environmental reporting is integrated into this annual report with limited assurance.

Operations serve people too: workforce development programs supported 700 professional staff through qualifications, gender equity targets brought women in leadership to 47 per cent, and the gender-sensitive policy review updated 40 procedures. Diversity and inclusion metrics are published to unit level. Health and wellbeing programs achieved 62 per cent staff participation, and the disaster resilience plan — tested by the November storm — kept both campuses operational.

## Governance

Council met eight times with 96 per cent attendance. Institutional integrity matters were handled under the revised framework: 12 research integrity cases (7 upheld), all published in summary. Policy transparency extends to publishing council minutes, and ethical leadership development is now mandatory for all senior appointments. Human rights due diligence covered our international partnerships and supply chains, with two contracts amended. Social equity, environmental sustainability, and economic impact are reported to council quarterly on a single integrated dashboard — the same dashboard this report draws from.

Professor Anaya Okafor, Vice-Chancellor
